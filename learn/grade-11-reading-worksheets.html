What your child will learn — Critical Analysis: Voice, Irony & Unreliable Narrators

  • Verbal irony says one thing, means another; situational irony is when the fire station burns down; dramatic irony is when YOU know what characters don't.
  • An unreliable narrator misleads through bias, naivety, or self-deception. Clues: contradictions, other characters' reactions, gaps in the story.
  • Voice = the fingerprint of the prose: diction, rhythm, what the narrator notices (and avoids noticing).
  • Read against the grain: What would another character say happened? What is this narrator protecting?

Sample worksheet questions

1. Find an example of verbal irony the narrator doesn't intend.
Answer: Examples: 'borrowing is the foundation of trust'; calling dish piles 'sculpture'
2. When does the new roommate arrive?
Answer: Tuesday
3. Why is this narrator unreliable?
Answer: Their claims ('excellent roommate') contradict the evidence they themselves reveal (mess, taking things, roommate fleeing)
4. How would Priya's version of events differ?
Answer: She'd describe a messy roommate who took her belongings without asking until she had to move out
5. Name two things the narrator 'borrowed' from Priya.
Answer: Headphones, laptop, and once her car
